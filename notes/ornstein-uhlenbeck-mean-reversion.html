Research note

Modeling a mean-reverting spread with the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process

April 2026 · ~7 min read

Pairs trading rests on one assumption: that the spread between two related assets wanders away from a fair level but is pulled back. The Ornstein–Uhlenbeck (OU) process is the simplest continuous-time model of exactly that pull. This note builds it from the stochastic differential equation up — its solution, stationary distribution, and half-life — then shows how it becomes an AR(1) regression you can actually estimate.

The equation

The OU process for a spread $X_t$ is the mean-reverting diffusion

dX_t = θ (μ − X_t) dt + σ dW_t, θ > 0

Read it as a force plus a kick. The drift $\theta(\mu - X_t)$ always points back toward the long-run level $\mu$ — above $\mu$ it is negative, below it is positive — with strength set by the mean-reversion speed $\theta$. The term $\sigma\,dW_t$ is the random shock that keeps knocking $X_t$ off $\mu$. The whole model is the tug-of-war between the two.

Solving it

This linear SDE has a closed-form solution. Multiply by the integrating factor $e^{\theta t}$ so the left side becomes an exact differential:

d(e^{θt} X_t) = e^{θt}(θμ dt + σ dW_t)
integrate from 0 to t, then multiply by e^(−θt):
X_t = μ + (X_0 − μ) e^(−θt) + σ ∫₀ᵗ e^(−θ(t−s)) dW_s

From this the first two moments follow directly:

E[X_t] = μ + (X_0 − μ) e^(−θt) → μ as t → ∞
Var[X_t] = (σ² / 2θ) (1 − e^(−2θt)) → σ²/2θ as t → ∞

The mean decays geometrically toward $\mu$ and the variance saturates. Because the noise integral is Gaussian, $X_t$ is normal at every horizon, and as $t\to\infty$ it forgets its start and settles into a stationary distribution

X_∞ ~ Normal( μ , σ² / 2θ )

That stationarity is the whole point: a stationary spread mean-reverts, so deviations are temporary and therefore potentially tradable. (Testing for it is what the Engle–Granger / ADF step in the pairs project does before any OU fitting.)

The half-life — the number that matters

How long does a deviation take to decay? Set the expected gap to half its initial size, $e^{-\theta t} = \tfrac12$:

t½ = ln 2 / θ

The half-life turns the abstract $\theta$ into a tradable timescale: roughly how many days until half a mispricing is expected to close. A short half-life (days) means frequent, exploitable round-trips; a long one means the signal is real but too sluggish to trade. This single number is what told me a pair was hopeless in practice — see the end.

From SDE to a regression you can estimate

Sample the exact solution on a fixed grid (say daily, $\Delta t = 1$). Conditioning on $X_t$, the next observation is

X_{t+1} = μ(1 − φ) + φ X_t + ε_t, φ = e^(−θ), ε_t ~ N(0, σ²(1−φ²)/2θ)

That is exactly an AR(1) process. So fitting OU is just ordinary least squares of $X_{t+1}$ on $X_t$ — regress, read off the intercept $c$ and slope $\phi$, and invert the map:

θ = −ln φ (mean-reversion speed)
μ = c / (1 − φ) (equilibrium level)
t½ = ln 2 / θ (half-life)
σ_eq = √( Var(ε) / (1 − φ²) ) (stationary std-dev)

The standardized spread $z_t = (X_t - \mu)/\sigma_{\text{eq}}$ is then the signal a pairs strategy trades: enter when $|z|$ is large, exit as it reverts toward zero. A whole continuous-time model collapses into a one-line regression — which is exactly why OU is the workhorse for mean-reversion.

Where this bit me — honestly

On the real EWA/EWC pair, the fitted half-life was about 83 days. Mathematically the spread mean-reverts; in trading terms it barely moves, producing only a handful of trades and no out-of-sample edge. The OU fit didn't just price the signal — it diagnosed why the strategy fails. Knowing the half-life up front would have set realistic expectations before any backtest.

That is the appeal of a model with this few parameters: every one of them means something. $\theta$ is a speed, $\mu$ a fair value, $\sigma$ a shock size, and their combination is a half-life you can sanity-check against how a real spread behaves.
