A from-scratch derivation of the Kelly criterion

March 2026 · ~7 min read

How much of your capital should you put on a favourable bet? Betting too little leaves growth on the table; betting too much courts ruin. The Kelly criterion answers this precisely by maximizing the long-run growth rate of wealth. Here it is, derived from first principles — first for a simple bet, then for continuous investing.

Why maximize expected log wealth?

The key idea is that wealth compounds multiplicatively. Over many bets, terminal wealth is a product of per-bet multipliers, $W_n = W_0\prod_{i=1}^n M_i$, so

ln(W_n / W_0) = Σ ln(M_i)
⇒ (1/n) ln(W_n/W_0) → E[ln M] (law of large numbers)

The long-run exponential growth rate is therefore the expected log of the multiplier, $g = \mathbb{E}[\ln M]$. Maximizing $g$ maximizes the rate at which wealth compounds — this is the growth-optimal objective, and it is what "Kelly" means. (It is also why we use logs: not an arbitrary utility choice, but the natural object when returns multiply.)

The discrete case: a single favourable bet

Bet a fraction $f$ of wealth on an outcome that pays net odds $b$-to-1 with probability $p$, and loses the stake with probability $q = 1-p$. The wealth multiplier is $1+bf$ on a win and $1-f$ on a loss, so the growth rate is

g(f) = p · ln(1 + b f) + q · ln(1 − f)

Differentiate, set to zero, and solve:

g'(f) = pb/(1 + bf) − q/(1 − f) = 0
⇒ pb(1 − f) = q(1 + bf)
⇒ pb − q = bf(p + q) = bf
⇒ f* = (pb − q) / b = p − q/b

(The second derivative is negative, so this is a maximum.) The formula is intuitive: bet your edge divided by the odds. For an even-money bet ($b=1$), $f^\* = p - q = 2p - 1$ — so a 55% edge says stake 10% of your bankroll. If the bet is not favourable ($pb \le q$), $f^\*\le0$: don't bet.

The continuous case: investing in a risky asset

Now allocate a fraction $f$ of wealth to a risky asset with expected return $\mu$ and volatility $\sigma$, the rest at the risk-free rate $r$. Model the asset as geometric Brownian motion; wealth then follows

dW/W = [r + f(μ − r)] dt + f σ dB_t

By Itô's lemma the log of wealth picks up the $-\tfrac12 (f\sigma)^2$ variance-drag term, giving an expected growth rate

g(f) = r + f(μ − r) − ½ f² σ²

This is concave in $f$; maximizing it is immediate:

g'(f) = (μ − r) − f σ² = 0
⇒ f* = (μ − r) / σ²

The optimal leverage is the excess return divided by variance — the continuous analogue of "edge over odds." Substituting back gives the maximum achievable growth rate a clean form:

g(f*) = r + ½ (μ − r)² / σ² = r + ½ · SR²

where $\mathrm{SR} = (\mu-r)/\sigma$ is the Sharpe ratio. So the best growth Kelly can extract scales with the square of the Sharpe ratio — a neat bridge to the Sharpe note: a higher Sharpe is worth more than linearly, but only if that Sharpe is real.

Why it's optimal — and why almost nobody bets full Kelly

Because $\tfrac1n\ln(W_n/W_0)\to g(f)$ almost surely, the Kelly bettor's wealth almost surely overtakes that of anyone following a materially different fixed strategy, for large enough $n$. That is a strong optimality result. Yet practitioners routinely bet a fraction of Kelly. Three good reasons:

  • Full Kelly is brutally volatile. The growth rate is flat near its peak, but variance keeps rising with $f$. Betting half-Kelly keeps about three-quarters of the growth while roughly halving volatility — a trade most people take gladly.
  • You don't know the true parameters. The formula needs $p,b$ (or $\mu,\sigma$) exactly. Plugging in noisy estimates makes you systematically over-bet, and over-betting past the growth-optimal point destroys growth fast. Parameter uncertainty alone argues for shrinking $f$.
  • Drawdowns and ruin. Kelly maximizes growth, not survival of a drawdown constraint. Anyone with a finite tolerance for losing (or a risk mandate) should bet less.
The one-line takeaway

Kelly says stake f* = (μ−r)/σ² — your edge per unit of risk — to compound fastest. In practice, bet a fraction of that, because the edge is estimated, not known.

This is also exactly the discipline behind position sizing in a real strategy: size by edge and variance, then haircut for the fact that both were measured with error — the same humility that made my pairs-trading backtest insist on out-of-sample testing before believing any number.
